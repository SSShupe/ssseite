Peter Ladouceur awoke an hour before sunrise. He turned on a dim lamp on his bedside table, pushed off the thick comforter, and got out of bed. In the chill of his bedroom he got on his knees and started his daily morning prayers.

He began by meditating, focusing on his breathing, in and out, fifteen or so minutes of centering, making himself ready to speak to God and receive God's word.

As soon as he began, Peter’s concentration on his breathing was interrupted by thoughts breaking into his consciousness, the same thoughts he had been facing the past few weeks: Why has God been silent? Has God heard me? Has God heard me and chosen not to respond? Peter noticed these thoughts but did not seize on them. He returned his focus to his breathing, following it, in-and-out, in-and-out, emptying his mind.

But the thoughts intruded: Has God forsaken me? Is God punishing me for my sin?

He used his meditation technique, noticing these thoughts but not seizing on them, trying to let them pass. But the thoughts persisted, leading him into a chain: Deborah, his desire, his weakness, his sin, his shame.

“My Lord, my God, I am weak. I am unworthy. Show me mercy, give me grace, forgive my offenses, let me live in your light once again.”

Silence. God did not reply. God did not forgive. From the day of his sin, Peter feared that God had exiled him from the kingdom of righteous.

Peter opened his eyes. In the dim light, his spartan room felt empty, apart. Peter rose to his feet and walked to the kitchen. He shivered, from the cold or from his fear that forgiveness would never come.

The days in Edmonton were getting longer, and the sunny blue sky on this early March day lifted Peter’s mood. He drove from his small house on the west side of the city to the lumber yard and store where he worked as a jack-of-all-trades: mostly sawing, planing, woodworking, sanding, but also delivering, customer service, anything that needed doing. He liked working with his hands, liked his co-workers, and made enough to support himself well. He’d been there almost fifteen years, having started when he was twenty.

After high school, Peter had considered going away to college. He was bright and his grades were good. But he hadn’t wanted to go into debt, and was wary of leaving the community where his family, friends, and church were located. An evangelical Christian, he was wary of the libertine hedonism he believed was the established religion in institutions of higher learning. So he stayed in Edmonton, working and participating actively in his church.

He arrived at work and parked his Toyota pickup around the back of the yard. Entering the building, Peter took in the familiar smell of lumber and sawdust, an odor he now found comforting. Before he could take off his coat, his supervisor Ron approached.

“The boss wants to see you.”

Peter’s eyes narrowed.

“It’s all good. No worries.”

Peter walked to the employees’ room, put his coat in his locker, and headed to the boss’s office, on a mezzanine overlooking the store. Melanie, the boss’s assistant, stood up from her desk as she saw Peter reach the top of the stairs. As usual, Peter thought, she had on too much makeup and wore an  immodest tight-fighting dress, which showed too much of her breasts and legs and indecently accentuated the roundness of her stomach and hips. Still, Peter had always liked her; she was clever and had a warm, genuine personality that drew Peter in despite her provocative appearance. 

“Hi Peter. He’s waiting for you. Follow me.”

They walked down a short hallway. Melanie knocked on a half-open door and stuck her head into the office. “Peter’s here.”

Bob Skelton, the boss, was a short fifty-year-old with a prominent beer belly and male pattern baldness, whose face nevertheless managed to convey an impish boyish handsomeness. He stood up from his desk as Peter entered the room.

“Peter! Peter! Good to see you. Have a seat.”

They shook hands and Peter sat.

“Ron told me yesterday that he’s leaving us to manage a lumberyard in Calgary.”

Peter was sorry to hear this. Ron was a good guy, fair and easy to work for.

“If you’re willing,” Bob continued, “I’d like you to take his place. It’s a good bump in salary and will give you management experience. You’re a good worker and have the potential to move up. No need to respond now. Take a few days and think about it. I think you’d do well.”

“Thank you, I didn’t expect this. Not at all. I appreciate your confidence in me. I like working here, I always have. I’ll think it over and let you know. By Friday, is that OK?”

“That’s fine. Ron’s here for another three weeks.”

Bob stood and reached out his hand. Peter stood and took it.

“I hope you’ll say yes,” Bob said. “You’re a good fit.”

They shook hands. “Thank you again,” Peter said, and left the office.

Melanie smiled and winked at Peter as he walked down the stairs to return to his post. She must have known, Peter thought, and that made him smile.

On his way, his phone chimed. A text, from Deborah: “It’s been a while. Lunch today maybe? I’ll buy. The Lunchbox at 12:30? Let me know, it would be good to talk.”

Peter stopped, his mind flooded by contradictory thoughts. Warmth. Deborah’s eyes, Deborah’s smile. Her laugh. The way she made him laugh. The color of her hair and skin. Affection. Attraction. Desire. Danger. Sin.

Today of all days.

He had distanced himself from Deborah since their last meeting – their last date, if he was being honest – a few weeks ago. She’d called twice and he hadn’t picked up. She’d texted a few times and he’d replied with short, neutral messages devoid of emotion or personality. Peter knew it wasn’t fair, knew he needed to address the situation. What, he thought to himself, was the right thing, the Christian thing, to do?

He replied to her text. “Yes. The Lunchbox at 12:30 is good. See you there.” Then a second: “Thank you, Deborah.”
